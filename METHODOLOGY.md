## Research Design

This study will employ a quantitative, computational–simulation research design. It will be
non-experimental in the laboratory sense, as no living organism will be manipulated or
subjected to treatment. Instead, a synthetic host xylem network will be generated *in
silico*, subjected to a mathematically modelled holoparasitic hydraulic sink, and then
repeatedly damaged under a Monte Carlo scheme in order to observe the conditions under which
the network fails. The response variable will be binary — the host network will either
collapse or survive in a given trial — and the quantity of interest will be the critical
vessel-loss threshold, denoted p_c, defined as the infestation load at which the probability
of collapse reaches fifty percent. This threshold is the percolation threshold of the host
network, a quantity that in network science marks the transition between a connected,
functional system and a fragmented, non-functional one (Artime et al., 2024), and which in
plant hydraulics corresponds to the tipping point beyond which water transport fails
catastrophically rather than gradually (Johnson et al., 2022).

At the level of the model, the design will be predictive and correlational. The independent
variables will be the haustorial sink strength, K_h; the fractal dimension of the host
venation, D; and the attachment geometry of the parasite. The dependent variable will be the
critical threshold p_c, which will be estimated separately for every combination of these
inputs. By systematically varying the independent variables and observing the resulting shift
in the threshold, the study will establish how parasite aggressiveness and host architecture
jointly determine vascular vulnerability.

A computational design is not merely convenient here; it is necessary. The central question —
at what fraction of drained vessels does a host network collapse? — cannot be answered through
observation or conventional experimentation, because answering it would require destroying the
same plant at every possible level of damage, thousands of times over, while holding all else
constant. Such an experiment is impossible *in vivo* but trivial *in silico*, and it is
precisely this impossibility that justifies the simulation-based approach the study will adopt.

## Materials and Instrumentation

The study will require no specialized laboratory equipment during its computational phase. All
simulations will be executed on a standard consumer workstation, requiring neither
high-performance computing facilities nor graphics-processing acceleration. The entire pipeline
will be implemented in Python 3.13, using NumPy for vectorized numerical operations and
pseudo-random number generation, SciPy for sparse linear algebra, statistical testing, and
numerical integration of the differential equation, NetworkX for graph construction and
topological analysis, scikit-learn for logistic regression, and Matplotlib for figure
generation. All package versions will be pinned in a `requirements.txt` file so that the
computational environment can be reconstructed exactly.

Reproducibility will be enforced by two design decisions. First, every parameter used anywhere
in the study will be centralized in a single frozen configuration module, so that no value can
be silently redefined in one script and not another. Second, every stochastic process in the
pipeline will draw from an explicitly seeded pseudo-random number generator. Consequently,
re-running any component of the study will reproduce the reported values exactly rather than
approximately, and any reader with access to the code will be able to verify every number
reported in this paper.

## Procedures

### Generation of the Synthetic Vascular Network

The host plant will be represented as a graph in which nodes correspond to vascular junctions
and edges correspond to xylem vessels. The network will be generated by recursive bifurcation
to a depth of nine generations, producing one thousand twenty-three nodes, of which one will be
the root, five hundred ten will be internal junctions, and five hundred twelve will be terminal
nodes representing leaf transpiration sites. At every bifurcation, the radii of the parent and
daughter vessels will be assigned according to Murray's Law (Murray, 1926), in which the cube of
the parent radius equals the sum of the cubes of the daughter radii, with the branching exponent
fixed at three. This exponent is not arbitrary: Skjegstad and Kirkegaard (2025), extending
Murray's Law to reticulate rather than strictly tree-like venation, recover optimal exponents
close to three for full-scale leaf networks, which supports the value adopted here. Each vessel
will then be assigned a hydraulic resistance following the Hagen–Poiseuille relation, in which
resistance is proportional to vessel length and inversely proportional to the fourth power of
vessel radius; the reciprocal of this resistance, the conductance, will be the quantity carried on
each edge and used by the solver.

A purely bifurcating tree, however, would be an inadequate model of a real leaf. Two thousand
forty-four anastomoses, or reticulating cross-links, will therefore be added to the tree,
yielding a final network of three thousand sixty-six vessels with a mean degree of approximately
six. This step is not cosmetic. A tree contains no cycles, and a network without cycles exhibits
no percolation transition whatsoever; the threshold this study sets out to measure would simply
not exist. Reticulation is thus a prerequisite of the entire investigation, and it is also the
biologically faithful choice, since real leaf venation is looped and mesh-like rather than
strictly branching. Ronellenfitsch and Katifori (2019) demonstrate that the reticulate,
hierarchically nested topology characteristic of plant venation emerges precisely as a trade-off
between transport efficiency, construction cost, and robustness to damage, and that loops are
what permit flow to be rerouted around an injured vein. Consistent with this, Mrad et al. (2021)
show that conduit redundancy and connectivity are decisive for xylem hydraulic function, and
Matos et al. (2024), surveying one hundred twenty-two fern and angiosperm species, find that
venation architecture is a strong predictor of a leaf's damage-resistance axis. A model of
parasite-induced vascular failure that omitted loops would therefore not merely be simplified; it
would be modelling a structure that does not fail in the way real leaves fail.

### Measurement of the Fractal Dimension

The space-filling character of the generated venation will be quantified by box-counting. The
network will be rasterized and overlaid with a series of square grids of progressively smaller
box size, ranging from sixteen to one thousand twenty-four boxes per side, and the number of
occupied boxes will be counted at each scale. The fractal dimension D will then be obtained as
the slope of the least-squares linear regression of the logarithm of the occupied-box count
against the logarithm of the reciprocal box size, fitted over the scale-invariant window in
which the relationship is genuinely linear. Because D is a dimensionless ratio of logarithms, it
is scale-invariant and will not require the network to be expressed in physical units.

The restriction of the fit to a windowed range is a deliberate methodological safeguard rather
than a convenience. Vishnu et al. (2023) demonstrate that traditional box-counting analysis of
leaves is prone to two systematic errors: the fractal properties of plant leaves hold only over a
limited range of scales, so that a regression fitted across all scales returns a biased estimate
of D, and the procedure is further subject to quantization error arising from arbitrary placement
of the counting grid. Restricting the regression to the genuinely linear window addresses the
first of these directly, and D will accordingly be reported here as one descriptor of venation
architecture rather than as a complete characterization of it.

### Hydraulic Solution and Haustorial Attachment

Water transport through the network will be solved at steady state by combining three physical
laws operating at three distinct levels of description. The Hagen–Poiseuille relation will supply
the conductance of each individual vessel from its radius and length. Darcy's law, in its
hydraulic Ohm's-law form, will govern the flow through each individual conduit, stating that
volumetric flow equals the conductance multiplied by the difference in water potential across the
vessel. Kirchhoff's Current Law will then enforce conservation of mass at every junction,
requiring that the total flow entering a node equal the total flow leaving it. Applying a Darcy
relation to every edge and a Kirchhoff balance to every node will assemble the entire network into
a single sparse linear system, in which the conductance Laplacian multiplied by the vector of
unknown water potentials equals a boundary vector. This system will be solved once for the water
potential at every node in the network. Dirichlet boundary conditions will be imposed by holding
the root node at a water potential of zero megapascals, representing the soil–stem interface, and
holding all terminal nodes at negative one megapascal, representing the transpirational pull
exerted at the leaves.

This resistor-network formulation is not a novel liberty taken by the present study but the
established approach in quantitative xylem hydraulics. Wason et al. (2021), modelling embolism
spread through the xylem network of grapevine, likewise apply the Hagen–Poiseuille law together
with conservation of mass to obtain a system of coupled equations relating flow rates and
pressure drops across every vessel, solved simultaneously in the manner of an electrical resistor
circuit. The present study adopts the same machinery and directs it, for the first time, at a
parasitic rather than a drought-induced sink.

The parasite will enter this system through a single additional term. The haustorium will be
modelled as a leak conductance, K_h, connecting a host node to a parasite reservoir held at a
water potential of negative three megapascals — a potential more negative than anywhere in the
healthy host, which is what will cause water to flow into the parasite rather than out of it. The
volumetric flow into the parasite will therefore be the product of K_h and the difference between
the host node's potential and the parasite's potential. This single parameter, K_h, will serve as
the primary independent variable of the study, because it is mathematically equivalent to
haustorial sink strength.

The coupling between hydraulics and topology will occur through cavitation. When the water
potential within a vessel is drawn below the cavitation threshold, fixed here at negative two
megapascals, an air bubble will nucleate and the vessel will embolize (Johnson et al., 2022). An
embolized vessel cannot conduct water and will therefore be deleted from the network entirely.
This rule is the conceptual hinge of the entire study: it converts a hydraulic event, namely
excessive tension, into a topological event, namely the removal of an edge from a graph. Once
vessels begin to disappear, the question of when the network fails becomes a question of
percolation rather than of fluid mechanics. That such failure is genuinely catastrophic rather
than gradual — that plants possess hydraulic tipping points beyond which conductance collapses
nonlinearly — is precisely the argument advanced by Johnson et al. (2022), and it is the premise
this study sets out to quantify for a parasitic sink.

### Monte Carlo Percolation

To determine the threshold, the infestation load ρ — the fraction of vessels drained or disabled —
will be swept across fifty values spanning zero to one. At each value of ρ, the corresponding
fraction of vessels will be disabled and the resulting network examined; this procedure will be
repeated twenty thousand times at every load level, yielding one million independent trials in
total. After each individual trial, the giant connected component will be computed, defined as the
largest surviving subnetwork that remains connected to the root and is therefore still capable of
receiving water. Collapse will be operationally defined as the giant connected component falling
below fifty percent of the original network size, on the grounds that a plant which cannot supply
half of its leaves has functionally failed as a transport system, regardless of whether it yet
appears wilted. Each trial will consequently be recorded as a binary outcome, coded one for
collapse and zero for survival. The use of the giant connected component as the order parameter of
network failure, and of its disappearance as the operational signature of collapse, follows
standard practice in percolation-based studies of network robustness (Artime et al., 2024).

Three attack modes will be compared in order to test whether the location of damage matters as much
as its quantity. In the random mode, vessels will be removed without regard to their position,
representing diffuse or systemic infestation. In the targeted-betweenness mode, vessels will be
removed in order of betweenness centrality, representing a parasite that taps the vascular backbone.
In the targeted-degree mode, vessels will be removed in order of degree, representing an attack on
the most highly connected junctions. This distinction is drawn directly from the network-robustness
literature, in which random failure and centrality-targeted attack are the two canonical damage
regimes and are known to yield markedly different critical thresholds on the same network
(Callaway et al., 2000; Artime et al., 2024). Applying the distinction to a parasitized plant
allows the study to ask whether *where* a haustorium attaches matters as much as *how much* it
drains.

For computational tractability, the Monte Carlo loop will operate entirely on integer-indexed SciPy
sparse matrices rather than on NetworkX graph objects, which will be converted once at the outset and
never touched inside the loop. This design decision is what will render one million trials feasible on
ordinary hardware.

### Threshold Estimation

The one million trials will produce one million pairs of an infestation load and a binary outcome.
These pairs will be fitted with a binary logistic regression, which models the probability of collapse
as a sigmoid function of load. The critical threshold p_c will then be defined as the point at which
this fitted sigmoid crosses fifty percent probability, which occurs at the negative ratio of the
regression intercept to the regression coefficient. Uncertainty in this estimate will be quantified by
percentile bootstrap resampling of the trials (Efron & Tibshirani, 1993), from which a ninety-five
percent confidence interval on p_c will be constructed.

### Parameter Sweep

Having established a single threshold, the study will then examine how that threshold responds to
changes in the parasite and in the host. A two-factor grid will be swept, varying the haustorial sink
strength K_h across five values from zero point three to two point five, and varying the reticulation
density of the host network so as to realize fractal dimensions spanning approximately one point three
five to one point four eight, while holding network size constant. The full hydraulic solution, Monte
Carlo simulation, and logistic threshold estimation will be re-executed independently within every cell
of this grid. The result will be a p_c surface — a two-dimensional map of host vulnerability as a joint
function of parasite aggressiveness and host architecture.

### Temporal Extension

The percolation model will yield a threshold but not a timetable; it will state how much loss causes
collapse, but not when that loss will accumulate. To supply the missing temporal dimension, the growth
of haustorial load will be modelled as a logistic process, in which the rate of change of ρ with respect
to time equals the product of a growth rate r, the current load ρ, and the remaining capacity one minus
ρ. This ordinary differential equation admits the closed-form solution in which ρ at time t equals the
reciprocal of one plus a constant multiplied by the exponential of negative r times t, where the
constant is determined by the initial infestation ρ₀. The logistic form is adopted because it is the
standard model for the temporal progression of a plant infestation: disease progress curves in plant
epidemiology are conventionally described by exponential, monomolecular, logistic, or Gompertz
population-dynamics models, of which the logistic is the canonical choice for a polycyclic process that
accelerates while susceptible tissue remains and saturates as that tissue is exhausted (Alves & Del
Ponte, 2021).

From this solution, two crossing times will be computed. The first, t_c, will be the time at which ρ
reaches the percolation threshold p_c, marking functional collapse of the transport network. The second,
t_wilt, will be the time at which ρ reaches the analytical fragmentation point f_c, marking the onset of
visible wilting. The interval between them, t_wilt minus t_c, will be the pre-symptomatic window: the
period during which the host is already functionally dead yet still outwardly healthy. To confirm that
the closed-form solution has been derived correctly, it will be verified against numerical integration
of the same differential equation using an adaptive Runge–Kutta solver at a relative tolerance of one
times ten to the negative ninth.

### Empirical Validation

To test the model against reality, venation and vessel data will be collected from five plant species,
comprising tomato, a legume, corn, mango, and hibiscus. These species have been chosen so that two
purposes are served simultaneously: several are documented hosts of the parasites modelled in this
study, and together they span a wide range of venation density, from the sparse parallel venation of
monocots to the densely reticulate venation of broad-leaved dicots. At least five individual plants will
be sampled per species, with three leaves taken from each plant, so that genuine standard errors can be
computed rather than inferred from a single specimen.

Two kinds of image will be obtained. Top-down venation photographs, acquired by backlighting or flatbed
scanning, will be used to measure the fractal dimension of real leaves using the identical windowed
box-counting algorithm applied to the synthetic networks, ensuring that the comparison is like for like.
Transverse cross-sections of stems and leaf petioles, taken at or near branch points so that a parent
vessel and its daughters appear together, will be used to measure the radii of real xylem vessels and
thereby estimate the empirical Murray's-law exponent, which the model assumes to be exactly three.

## Risk and Safety Management

### Ethical Considerations

This study will involve no human participants and no animal subjects. The computational phase will
involve no living organisms of any kind, since the host plant will exist only as a mathematical graph.
Institutional ethical clearance for research on human or animal subjects is therefore not applicable to
this study. The empirical phase will involve only cultivated plant material and will likewise raise no
questions of animal or human welfare, although the collection protocol described below has been designed
to avoid any impact on protected species or protected habitats.

### Risks in the Computational Phase

Although the computational phase will present no physical hazard, it will not be risk-free, and the risks
it does present have been identified and will be controlled in advance.

The most serious hazard will be the loss or corruption of code and results. This will be controlled by
maintaining the entire project under Git version control, with all generated data and figures written to
dedicated output directories and committed to the repository, so that no result exists only in volatile
memory or on a single machine.

A second risk is that results may prove irreproducible, which would invalidate the study regardless of the
correctness of its conclusions. This will be controlled by the frozen configuration module and the seeded
random number generators described earlier, which together will guarantee that any run of the pipeline
reproduces its reported output exactly.

A third risk is silent numerical failure, in which the sparse linear system becomes singular or fails to
converge and returns a plausible but meaningless answer. This will be controlled by including a small
grounding term in the system matrix, which will keep the matrix non-singular even if a portion of the
network becomes detached during severe infestation, and by an automated smoke-test suite that will be
executed after any change to the code.

A fourth risk is the interruption of long-running jobs, since the full one-million-trial simulation will
not be instantaneous. This will be controlled by staging the pipeline and recording the last successfully
completed stage to disk, so that an interrupted run may be resumed rather than restarted from the
beginning.

The fifth and most consequential risk is not technical but intellectual: the risk of overstating the
certainty of an uncalibrated model. Because no published study reports an empirical value for haustorial
volumetric flux, the sink-strength parameter K_h cannot be fitted to a measured quantity. The controlling
measure to be adopted is to sweep K_h across a range rather than assert any single value for it, to
accompany every reported estimate with its confidence interval, and to state the limitations of the model
explicitly and unprompted in every written and oral output of this research. This risk is treated as the
most severe of all, because a computational result presented with more confidence than it has earned does
more damage to the scientific record than one that is simply wrong.

Finally, prolonged work at a computer terminal will carry the ordinary risks of eye strain and repetitive
strain injury, which will be managed through scheduled breaks and attention to workstation ergonomics.

### Risks in the Empirical Validation Phase

The empirical phase will introduce the only physical hazards in this study, and the controls described
below will be in place before any specimen is collected or sectioned.

The primary hazard will be laceration from the razor blades and scalpels used to prepare hand sections of
stems and petioles. To control this hazard, all cutting will be performed away from the body on a stable
cutting surface, blades will be held in a proper blade holder rather than by hand wherever possible, and
only members who have been trained in the technique will perform sectioning. A first-aid kit will be
present at the workstation throughout, and all used blades will be disposed of in a rigid sharps container
and never in general waste.

Glass microscope slides and coverslips will present a secondary risk of cuts from breakage. Slides will be
handled by their edges, coverslips will never be forced into position, and any breakage will be disposed of
in the same sharps container.

Plant material itself will present a risk of sap irritation and allergic contact dermatitis, and in some
species a risk of latex sensitivity. Nitrile gloves will therefore be worn during sectioning, hands will be
washed after handling any specimen, no plant material will be ingested under any circumstances, and species
known to produce strongly irritant sap will be avoided in favour of the safe alternatives already identified
in the sampling plan.

Field and garden collection will present the ordinary risks of sun exposure, insect bites, and uneven
terrain. Collection will be conducted in pairs and never alone, closed footwear and sun protection will be
used, and specimens will be collected only from cultivated plots or otherwise permitted areas. No protected
species will be collected and no protected land will be entered.

Microscope use will present minor risks of eye strain and of electrical hazard from the illuminator, which
will be managed by following standard laboratory microscope protocol and by inspecting cords for damage
before each use.

Finally, if leaf-clearing reagents are used to render specimens translucent, they will present a risk of
chemical irritation. This risk will be avoided by design wherever possible: the priority dataset, namely the
top-down venation photographs used to measure fractal dimension, can be obtained by backlighting or flatbed
scanning alone, requiring no blades and no chemicals whatsoever. Should any reagent nonetheless prove
necessary, its safety data sheet will be consulted, work will be conducted in a ventilated area, and gloves
and eye protection will be worn. It is worth stating explicitly that the sharps hazard will arise only in the
secondary cross-sectioning task, and that the study's most important empirical measurement can be obtained
with nothing more dangerous than a camera.

### Data Integrity

All raw simulation outputs will be retained in unmodified form, and no data point will be discarded at any
stage. Where a particular parameter combination fails to produce a defined threshold — as will occur, for
example, when a network is so densely reticulated that a weak sink cannot collapse it at all — that cell will
be reported as undefined rather than being omitted from the record or filled by interpolation. Reporting an
absence of collapse as an absence of data would misrepresent the model's behaviour precisely where that
behaviour is most interesting.

## Data Analysis

All inferential procedures in this study will be conducted at a level of significance of 0.05, and all
interval estimates will be reported at ninety-five percent confidence.

### Primary Statistical Tools

The foundational procedure will be Monte Carlo simulation, which will generate the study's raw data: one
million binary observations of whether the host network collapsed at a given infestation load. These
observations will then be analysed by binary logistic regression, which is the appropriate model because each
trial is a Bernoulli outcome and the quantity being estimated is a probability. The fitted regression will
yield the collapse-probability sigmoid, from which the critical threshold will be obtained as the negative
ratio of the intercept to the coefficient. Uncertainty in that threshold will be quantified by percentile
bootstrap resampling of the trials, which will produce the ninety-five percent confidence interval to be
reported alongside every estimate of p_c.

The fractal dimension of the network will be obtained by least-squares linear regression performed in
logarithmic coordinates, in which the logarithm of the occupied-box count is regressed on the logarithm of the
reciprocal box size and the resulting slope is taken as D. Because a single generated network is only one
realization of a stochastic process, D will not be reported as a single number but as a distribution: thirty
independent networks will be generated from thirty different random seeds, and the mean, standard deviation,
and ninety-five percent confidence interval of D across these seeds will be computed using the Student's *t*
distribution.

### Statistical Tools for Model Validation

A threshold estimate would be worthless if the model that produced it did not fit the data, and so several
procedures will be used to interrogate the fit itself rather than the result.

The goodness of fit of the logistic model will be assessed by McFadden's pseudo-R², for which values above
zero point four conventionally indicate an excellent fit, and by the ordinary coefficient of determination
computed at the level of the fifty density bins, comparing the observed proportion of collapses in each bin
against the proportion predicted by the fitted model. A Hosmer–Lemeshow chi-square goodness-of-fit test will
also be reported, though it will be interpreted with explicit caution: at sample sizes on the order of one
hundred thousand, even trivially small deviations from the fitted model attain statistical significance, and so
the effect-size measures will be treated as the honest indicators of fit quality while the chi-square will be
reported for completeness.

Because the central claim of this study is that vascular collapse is a genuine phase transition rather than a
gradual decline — a claim consonant with the tipping-point behaviour independently argued for plant hydraulics by
Johnson et al. (2022) — two procedures will be used to test that claim directly rather than assume it. The first
will be finite-size scaling analysis, in which the threshold and the width of the transition are estimated
separately for networks of increasing size, from six to ten generations. A true phase transition sharpens as the
system grows, approaching a step function in the infinite-size limit; if the transition width failed to shrink
with increasing size, the apparent threshold would be an artifact of the finite network rather than a physical
phenomenon. The second will be estimation of the critical exponent, obtained by regressing the logarithm of the
giant component fraction against the logarithm of the distance from the threshold within the near-threshold
scaling window. The recovery of a clean power law will confirm a continuous transition, and the fitted exponent
will be compared against the known values for the mean-field and two-dimensional lattice universality classes
(Artime et al., 2024).

The simulation engine itself will be validated against closed-form theory using the Molloy–Reed and Callaway
criterion (Molloy & Reed, 1995; Callaway et al., 2000), which predicts the fragmentation point analytically from
the network's degree distribution as one minus the reciprocal of kappa minus one, where kappa is the ratio of the
second moment to the first moment of the degree distribution. Agreement between the simulated fragmentation point
and this analytical prediction will demonstrate that the Monte Carlo machinery is producing correct physics and
not merely plausible numbers.

The realism of the synthetic architecture will be tested by a one-sample *t*-test comparing the distribution of
the model's fractal dimension across thirty seeds against published measurements of real leaf venation, thereby
establishing whether the generated networks are statistically consistent with actual leaves. The ranking of the
five parasite profiles by their thresholds will be assessed by examining whether the bootstrap confidence
intervals of adjacent parasites overlap; where they do not, the ordering will be established as a real effect
rather than sampling noise. Finally, the correctness of the closed-form solution to the differential equation
will be confirmed by computing the maximum absolute deviation between it and a numerically integrated solution of
the same equation.

### Statistical Tools for the Empirical Validation Phase

The fractal dimension measured on real leaves will be compared against the model's fractal dimension by
one-sample *t*-test, conducted separately for each species, in order to determine whether the synthetic
architecture is a statistically defensible representation of real venation. One-way analysis of variance will be
used to test whether fractal dimension differs significantly across the five sampled species, which is necessary
to establish that the intended venation-density gradient genuinely exists in the collected specimens. The
empirical Murray's-law exponent will be estimated by log-log regression of daughter vessel radii on parent radii,
and the null hypothesis that this exponent equals three — the value assumed throughout the model, and the value
recovered for reticulate leaf networks by Skjegstad and Kirkegaard (2025) — will be tested directly. Finally,
Pearson correlation will be used to relate the measured fractal dimension of each species to the threshold that
the model predicts for it. This last procedure will be the most important of the four, because it will constitute
a direct empirical test of the study's central prediction: that more densely veined hosts resist parasitic
collapse for longer, a prediction consistent with the association between venation architecture and
damage resistance reported by Matos et al. (2024).

### Justification of the Analytical Choices

Three choices in the analysis warrant explicit defence.

Logistic regression will be used in preference to simply reading the fifty-percent point off the observed collapse
curve because each trial is a Bernoulli outcome, making the logistic model the natural probabilistic description
of the data. Fitting the entire sigmoid will use all one million observations to estimate the threshold rather
than relying only on the comparatively small number of trials that happen to fall in the bins nearest the crossing
point, and it will yield a closed-form expression for the threshold whose uncertainty can be propagated
rigorously.

Bootstrap resampling will be used in preference to a parametric confidence interval because the threshold is a
ratio of two estimated regression parameters, and the sampling distribution of such a ratio is not normal. The
bootstrap makes no distributional assumption and is therefore the defensible choice, whereas a parametric interval
would rest on an assumption known to be false.

The fractal dimension will be reported as a distribution across thirty seeds rather than as a single value because
any one generated network is a single draw from a random process. Reporting the mean with a confidence interval
will separate the systematic architecture of the model from run-to-run stochastic variation, and it is moreover
the only form in which the model's dimension can be subjected to a *t*-test against real leaf measurements.

## Limitations of the Methodology

The limitations of this methodology are stated here in advance rather than conceded later under questioning.

The generated network will be two-dimensional, whereas real plant vasculature is three-dimensional; Wason et al.
(2021) note that the three-dimensional complexity of real xylem networks means the functional consequences of
intervessel connection are still imperfectly understood, and the present model necessarily inherits that
simplification. The sink-strength parameter K_h will remain uncalibrated, because no published study reports a
haustorial volumetric flux against which it could be fitted; it will therefore be swept across a range rather than
assigned a value, and this should be understood as a documented gap in the existing literature rather than a
shortcut taken in the present method. Root parasites will be approximated rather than modelled directly, since the
network will represent the shoot vasculature with the root condensed into a single source node; root attachment
will consequently be modelled as an attack on the root-proximal backbone, and a full root-system model remains
future work. The hydraulic solver will assume steady state, so that transient phenomena such as diurnal cycling
and embolism refilling will not be represented. The parameters of the differential equation, namely the growth
rate and the initial infestation, will be illustrative rather than calibrated, which means that the shape and
existence of the pre-symptomatic window will constitute the finding, while its duration in days will not.

The fractal dimension must also be interpreted with restraint. As Vishnu et al. (2023) show, box-counting analysis
of leaves is limited by the narrow range of scales over which real venation is genuinely self-similar and by
quantization error in grid placement, with the consequence that D is a coarse descriptor of venation architecture
rather than a complete one. For this reason D is reported here as a distribution with a confidence interval rather
than as a single value, and it is treated as one architectural parameter among several rather than as the sole
determinant of host resilience — a caution reinforced by Matos et al. (2024), who find that venation traits at
different spatial scales contribute to different functional axes and that multiple alternative network designs can
achieve comparable function.

Finally, and most importantly, the numerical value of the threshold obtained in this study will be a property of
this particular network under these particular parameters. What will be robust to changes in those parameters is
the existence and the sharpness of the threshold, and the ordering of the parasite profiles relative to one
another; the third decimal place of the threshold itself will not be, and no claim will be made upon it.
