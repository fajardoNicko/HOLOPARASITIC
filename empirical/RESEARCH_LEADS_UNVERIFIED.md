## https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/plant-science/articles/10.3389/fpls.2013.00056/full
*quality: primary · published: 2013-03-18*

- **[central]** The hydraulically weighted (hydraulic) diameter D_H is defined with fourth-power weighting derived from the Hagen-Poiseuille law, and represents the single mean diameter all vessels would need to have to give the same overall conductivity — this is the reporting convention the team needs for vessel-radius inputs to a resistor-network xylem model.
  > Based on the Hagen–Poiseuille law the diameter scales to the fourth power of the conductance. ... D_H calculated following Equation 4 represents the mean diameter that all of the vessels in a stem would have in order to correspond to the overall conductivity.
- **[supporting]** For a hand-sectioning fallback measurement protocol, the paper prescribes a concrete minimum sample size of 50 vessels measured per sample, with the number of sectors/vessels scaled to the variability of conduit size and density.
  > It is recommended that a minimum of 50 vessels is measured per sample, although the number of sectors and vessels depends on variation of conduit size and density.
- **[supporting]** Because vessels in transverse section are typically elliptical rather than circular, a simple equivalent-circle diameter is not always adequate and an arithmetic/elliptical-corrected diameter may be more accurate — a measurable source of error if the team measures lumen diameters from their own microscope sections.
  > Since conduits are frequently elliptical and rarely perfect circles as seen in transverse sections, it is sometimes more accurate to calculate the arithmetic diameter.
- **[supporting]** The paper names specific, obtainable image-analysis software for quantifying conduits from anatomical sections — WinCELL, ROXAS, Image Pro Plus, and ImageJ — with ImageJ singled out for its plugin/macro ecosystem, making ImageJ/Fiji the free route for an undergraduate team.
  > WinCELL, ROXAS, Image Pro Plus, and ImageJ
- **[supporting]** Vessel density is standardized as the number of conduits per 1 mm2 of transverse section, giving the team a unit convention to match when pulling published values or measuring their own sections.
  > Vessel density is quantified as the average number of conduits per 1 mm2.

## https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0367253023000907
*quality: primary · published: 2023-07 (Flora vol. 304, art. 152300; online 2023. SSRN preprint posted January 2023)*

- **[central]** Leaf fractality holds only over a narrow range of scales, so the standard linear-regression fit of log N(r) vs log(1/r) used in box-counting is not valid across the full scale range — this is the paper's core methodological warning and directly supports the research question's 'limited scale-invariance window' pitfall. Any box-counting D the team computes must be accompanied by an explicit statement of the scaling window over which the regression was fitted, and evidence of linearity within it.
  > Fractal properties of plant leaves are restricted over a limited scale, causing the failure of the linear regression-based D estimates.
- **[central]** Box-counting on leaf images carries quantization error from arbitrary placement of the box grid and from mis-tallying boxes at a given size, meaning a single grid origin gives a biased D; the fix implied is grid-offset averaging / repeated placements rather than one pass.
  > Arbitrary grid placement and miscounting the boxes of a specific size to cover the leaves are also subject to quantization errors.
- **[central]** Traditional box-counting D is area-dependent and cannot capture lacunarity (gap/void structure) in leaf images, so D alone is an insufficient descriptor and should be reported alongside lacunarity — relevant to the research question's request for descriptors that must accompany box-counting (vein density, areole statistics, loopiness).
  > Traditional box-counting fractal analysis is area dependent and fails to capture the lacunarity.
- **[supporting]** Across mature, healthy flat leaves of 61 plant species collected in Trivandrum, Kerala, India and analysed with HarFA and ImageJ, the box-counting fractal dimension D showed no significant variation between leaf forms — i.e. D is a weak discriminator between species/leaf shapes. Caveat for this project: the analysis was of whole-leaf images (bulk and boundary), NOT of extracted venation networks, so the paper supplies no venation-network D benchmark values; it is a pitfalls source, not a benchmark-value source.
  > Mature and healthy flat leaves of 61 plant species were collected ... D did not show significant variation to differentiate the leaf forms.

- **[central]** The Zenodo record 13300782 (Matos et al., 'Macroevolutionary trends in leaf venation network architecture', v1, 12 Aug 2024, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.13300782) is an open-access dataset under CC BY 4.0 requiring no registration, containing cleared-leaf images plus CNN vein segmentations for 1,000 leaf specimens (880 extant, 120 fossil) spanning 784 genera, 325 families, 90 orders — downloadable as ~7 GB of ZIPs (CNN-part1.zip 986.4 MB, CNN-part2.zip 878.0 MB, CNN-part3.zip 442.3 MB, Costa_Rica_samples.zip 779.5 MB, Ecuador_samples_part1-3.zip ~2.1 GB, UCMP_samples_part1-2.zip ~1.2 GB, Masks, data.zip 319.4 MB, Scripts, Results, Figures).
  > Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International
- **[central]** The image archives include binary vein segmentations already in graph-ready raster form — veins as white pixels on black — which the team can skeletonize directly instead of segmenting raw photographs; segmentation used LeafVeinCNN versions 1.0.7 and 2.12, and LeafVeinCNN 1.0.7 software plus manual are separately archived and downloadable at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.4007731 (a named, obtainable NEED-3 tool).
  > leaf image segmentation (i.e. conversion of a coloured leaf cleared image into a binary image where veins pixels are shown in white and non-veins pixels are shown in black) was done automatically using LeafVeinCNN program versions 1.0.7 (software and manual available at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.4007731) and 2.12
- **[central]** Every specimen carries a recorded physical scale (image resolution determined per image, reported as 0.003-0.061 pixels mm-1 extant and 0.006-0.179 pixels mm-1 fossil — units as printed are almost certainly inverted and should read mm per pixel), and the pipeline resolves veins down to >=10 micrometres width, fine enough to recover minor veins and closed areoles for percolation/loop analysis.
  > Image pre-processing involved cropping the leaf image, preparing masks to delineate leaf boundaries, and determining image resolution, which ranged from 0.003 to 0.061 pixels mm-1 in extant species and from 0.006 to 0.179 pixels mm-1 in fossil species.
- **[supporting]** The deposit contains loop/areole topology descriptors already computed for all 1,000 taxa across three vein size classes — vein density (VD), minimum spanning tree ratio (MST), loop elongation ratio (ER), loop circularity ratio (CR), plus hierarchical loop decomposition in HLD_final_1000.csv and ace_scaled_FINAL.csv — giving published benchmark ranges of loopiness and vein density against which the team's own extracted networks can be sanity-checked.
  > Overall, venation networks evolved from having fewer veins with convoluted loops, to having more veins, straighter loops, and more internal free-ending veins.
- **[supporting]** The extant cleared-leaf imagery aggregates the major named cleared-leaf archives the team is seeking (Smithsonian, UC Berkeley, Wilf collection) plus new field collections from Costa Rica, Ecuador and Ghana, making this one record a licence-clear route to those collections' imagery; however the analyses shipped are purely macroevolutionary (phylogenetic ridge regression, disparity-through-time, GLS in seven R scripts), with no percolation, resistor-network, or hydraulic-conductance modelling on the extracted networks.
  > Extant species from cleared leaf collections (Smithsonian, UC Berkeley, Wilf collection)

## https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:de65fc07-4b8f-4277-a6c4-82836afbdeb3
*quality: primary · published: 2018-08-21*

- **[central]** The Oxford Research Archive record 'Leaf venation networks of Bornean trees: images and hand-traced segmentations' (Blonder et al.) is a live, open-access dataset released under the Open Data Commons Attribution License (ODC-BY), downloadable without registration as a single file, Blonder_B_2018_Leaf_venation_networks_Data.zip, of 24.3 GB — so the URL resolves and the data is genuinely obtainable today.
  > Blonder_B_2018_Leaf_venation_networks_Data.zip — 24.3GB; Licence: Open Data Commons Attribution License (ODC-BY); Open access; file freely downloadable
- **[central]** The dataset contains both raw leaf venation images and matched hand-traced (human-annotated) segmentations in PNG format, and is explicitly stated to be usable for training machine-learning algorithms — i.e. it supplies ground-truth vein masks, not just photographs, which is what a graph-extraction/segmentation pipeline needs.
  > The dataset contains images and tracings of leaf venation networks obtained from tree species in Malaysian Borneo. These images are suitable for biological analysis and/or for training of machine-learning algorithms.
- **[central]** The ORA landing page does not report image resolution, microns-per-pixel, magnification, scale bars, or the number of leaves/species included, so whether minor veins and areoles are resolvable and whether absolute vein density or scale-calibrated box-counting fractal dimension can be computed cannot be confirmed without downloading the 24.3 GB archive and inspecting its internal metadata.
  > No information about image resolution, microns per pixel, magnification, number of leaves, or number of species is provided in the visible content.
- **[supporting]** The material is from tropical tree species sampled in 2015 from eight permanent forest plots in Sabah, Malaysian Borneo, part of the Global Ecosystems Monitoring (GEM) network — meaning it contains no data on the team's five target species (tomato, legume, maize, mango, hibiscus) and would serve as a generic venation-topology or segmentation-training corpus rather than a species match.
  > Samples were obtained from eight permanent forest plots that are part of the Global Ecosystems Monitoring network...
- **[supporting]** The deposit is dated 2018 (deposited 21 August 2018) and the ORA record lists no associated journal publication, so the commonly cited 'Blonder et al. (2019)' framing should be treated as unconfirmed for this record; the citable identifier is the ARK ark:/29072/ora_de65fc074b8f4277a6c482836afbdeb3.
  > Publication/Deposit Date: 2018 (deposited August 21, 2018) ... No specific publication citation provided in the record. ARK: ark:/29072/ora_de65fc074b8f4277a6c482836afbdeb3

## https://nph.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/nph.16830
*quality: primary · published: 2020-08-31 (online early); issue December 2020 — New Phytologist 228(6): 1796–1810*

- **[central]** The paper's underlying venation dataset is openly downloadable with no registration: the article states the venation data, trait subset and phylogeny live at https://doi.org/10.5287/bodleian:QR11d1PD2 (resolves to Oxford ORA record uuid:2103b7d7-d7c9-47f4-9175-39be64e2908d; verified live). It contains FILE_S1.1_data_joined.csv.gz (408.2 MB), FILE_S1.2_data_traits.csv.gz (83.4 KB) and FILE_S1.3_phylo.new (10.5 KB) under an Open Data Commons Attribution (ODC-BY) licence; R code is at https://doi.org/10.5287/bodleian:E9JP2gjyP and the full trait dataset at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3247631. The Wiley DOI page itself returned HTTP 402 to automated fetch, but the CC BY 4.0 published PDF is free at https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:2df095e5-83be-495b-8f1d-8607b0a3e38f.
  > Venation data, subsetted trait data, and the phylogeny are available at https://doi.org/10.5287/bodleian:QR11d1PD2. R code to replicate all analyses using these files is available at https://doi.org/10.5287/bodleian:E9JP2gjyP. Venation network image data are available in Blonder et al. (2019). Segmented venation network images (like those shown in Fig. 2) are available in Xu et al. (2020). Algorithms to analyze images and to calculate multiscale statistics are available in Xu et al. (2020). The full trait dataset (including numerous other variables) is available at https://doi.org/10.5281/zeno
- **[central]** The paper specifies an exact, reproducible imaging benchmark for cleared-leaf venation work: chemically cleared and stained ~2 cm2 leaf sections, imaged on an Olympus BX43 compound microscope with a x2 apochromat objective and a 3840 x 2748 pixel Olympus SC100 colour camera, ~16 fields stitched, giving a final resolution of 595 pixels per millimetre (i.e. ~1.68 um/pixel). Final networks spanned 94-180 mm2 (1-99% quantiles) with minimum vein radii of 13-68 um. This is the resolution scale needed to resolve minor veins and areoles, and it is well above what a consumer flatbed scanner delivers.
  > Samples were mounted on glass slides and imaged using a compound microscope (Olympus BX43) with a x2 apochromat objective and a color camera (3840 x 2748 pixel resolution; Olympus SC100; Southend-on-Sea, UK). Approximately 16 image fields were then stitched together to obtain a full image of each sample (resolution 595 px mm-1).
- **[central]** Vein segmentation was done by a named, published deep-learning pipeline the team can obtain: an ensemble of six U-Net convolutional neural networks trained on 686,881,432 hand-segmented pixels, which then algorithmically segmented 32,815,701,653 pixels at high accuracy (Fbeta2 mean = 0.95 +/- 0.03 SD). The paper points to Xu et al. (2020) for the algorithms; that software is distributed as LeafVeinCNN on Zenodo (https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.4007731, v1.0.7, CC-BY-4.0, Windows .exe standalone and MATLAB .mlappinstall, ~1.1 GB each, plus a 72 MB manual) by Fricker, Blonder and Xu. Note: veins wider than ~500 um still had to be segmented manually, so the CNN alone does not fully recover major veins.
  > An ensemble of six convolutional neural networks (CNNs) was developed, with each network implemented via a U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015). ... The algorithms sometimes under-segmented large veins that were not included in the validation dataset. Any veins with widths greater than c. 500 um were also manually segmented throughout the entire sample.
- **[central]** The pipeline produces a true spatial GRAPH that explicitly recovers loops/areoles and their boundary vein segments, and the study runs an iterative EDGE-REMOVAL procedure on those real extracted networks - sequentially deleting the smallest-radius boundary vein segment and recomputing network statistics after each fusion. This is the closest published precedent to a percolation/edge-removal analysis on a real leaf network, but it is a hierarchical loop-decomposition ordered by vein radius, NOT a percolation-threshold or giant-component analysis: no p_c, no giant-component size, and no random edge removal are reported.
  > Spatial graph representations of the networks were extracted from each segmented image. Next, all unique loops and their boundary vein segments were identified. Unclosed loops (on the boundary of the sample) were removed from the analysis. ... After calculating these statistics, the network was iteratively pruned, and loops fused, by sequential removal of the smallest remaining boundary vein segment. After each fusion event, network statistics were re-calculated, along with the minimum vein radius (rmin, um).
- **[supporting]** Despite having ~639 real extracted venation graphs with per-segment vein radii, the authors did NOT build any hydraulic model: hydraulic conductance is explicitly flagged as unmeasured, and the only descriptors reported are vein density (VD), loop elongation ratio (ER), loop circularity ratio (CR) and minimum spanning tree ratio (MST) - no box-counting fractal dimension, no Hagen-Poiseuille or resistor-network simulation, and no embolism-spread modelling. Scale coverage was also truncated to rmin 0-0.2 mm to avoid undersampling bias, a concrete example of the limited scale-invariance window that undermines box-counting on leaves.
  > transport. For example, hydraulic conductance (unmeasured) might be more closely linked to water transport functions, whereas photosynthetic capacity would be more linked to carbon uptake functions (measured). The role of venation in predicting hydraulic conductance appears to be complex, even at single scales.
